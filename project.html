Project

Can reduced meat consumption trend today generate considerable environmental impact?

Introduction and aims

Greenhouse gas emission from agricultural sector has grown over time, worsening environmental problem. Research suggests that this is 26% of total greenhouse gas emission. This implies that if we can change our diet in more environmentally friendly way, we may be able to improve environmental problems significantly. This research aims to analyse how diet of people in the UK has changed, and whether this reduced negative impact on environment. The research will particularly focus on meat consumption, as it produces about a half of total agricultural sector emission and has the biggest potential for reduction with high emission per kilogram.

Motivation for change

According to the survey, about a half of population in the UK has attempted to change their diet to improve health or sustainability. This attempt involves activities like eating less meat or following diet with no animal products.

Has it changed?

Although survey year does not match, data above will show Britonsâ€™ willingness and attempts to change diet. In relation to that, such attempts are reflected on the change in daily meat intake. Overall meat consumption in gram has fallen. There has been gradual drop in consumption of red meat while consumption of white meat has increased slightly. Red meat consumption drop would be due to health concern and environmental impact of red meat, particularly beef with high per kilogram impact. White meat consumption increase would be due to less media exposure of health issues from white meat and lower per kilogram impact compared to red meat. White meat consumption increase would be due to less emphasis of health issues from white meat and lower per kilogram impact compared to red meat.

Environmental impact

As a result, individuals in 2019 saved about 0.76kg greenhouse gas, 3m^2 land, and 15.7 litres of fresh water every day on average, compared to people in 2008. This is mainly driven by drop in red meat eating, particularly beef. As per kilogram impact of beef is high, it cancelled out the impact of other meat increase, especially in greenhouse gas emission.

Further analysis

However, not all countries have similar trend as in the UK. If we compare developed and developing countries, we can find similar pattern only appears in developed countries with reduced beef and sheep meat consumption and increased poultry consumption. In contrast, meat consumption in developing countries has risen for all meat types. We can deduce that this is because of different absolute amount consumed as shown in the chart.

Along with phenomenon that people tend to eat more meat as their income rises, data indicates that meat consumption per capita will further rise until these countries reach certain level of income and meat consumption. Therefore, if we view globally, such environmentally friendly change is only limited to some countries. To make considerable difference, we need further measure such as larger reduction per capita or globalisation of such trend.

Besides, world population is expected to rise for next few decades. This will cancel out individual effort in such trend, so we need more saving to make an impact.

Conclusion

To conclude, there has been a change in meat consumption of people in the UK, which improved greenhouse gas emission, land use, and water use per capita. This was driven by drop in beef (red meat) consumption, implying change in composition of meat eating can minimize individual impact effectively. However, this is not enough to generate meaningful difference overall due to population increase and other countries with different trends.
